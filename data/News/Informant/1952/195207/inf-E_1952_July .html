Around the World with Jehovah’s Witnesses

From House to House

Going from house to house is a basic theocratic activity. How can we say to the prisoners “Go forth’’’ unless we first find them, and what better way is there of finding them than going from house to house? —Acts 5:42; 20:20.

In going from house to house do we always make sure to see that every home receives a witness? In Australia, as shown by our 1952 “Yearbook”, because a publisher went back to witness to a home previously missed, one of the Lord’s other sheep, though physically deaf, heard the good news of the Kingdom and took his stand. Now he is helping others to hear spiritually while he confidently looks forward to receiving his physical sense of hearing in the new world. (81)

And while attending assemblies let us not neglect the house-to-house activity. In the Saar, as a result of house-to-house activity during a circuit assembly interest was found at a home; which, being followed up soon, became a home Bible study with ten taking part. By the time the next assembly came around, six months later, six of these were immersed. (139)

We may be engaged in the house-to-house activity, witnessing to one householder after another without finding any interest and still it may be that appreciative ears are listening. That is what one Guatemalan publisher experienced: no interest at the doors but much interest on the part of construction workers on the street who had overheard his preaching. (148) Yes, house-to-house activity brings results. Are you engaging in it regularly?

“Buying Out the Opportune Time”

At Ephesians 5:16 (NW) the apostle Faul counsels us to 'buy out the opportune time for ourselves because the days are wicked’. Surely never before have the days been so wicked as now nor has the need to buy out the opportune time on the part of God’s servants been as urgent as in this pre-Armageddon era with the eternal destiny of so many at stake. —Ezek. 3:17-21.

A good way to buy out the opportune time is by witnessing while traveling. The missionaries sent to Ethiopia found the first few months there rather difficult. Then one day as one of them was walking along a road he accosted an Ethiopian youth and witnessed to him. The young man showed keen interest and he, together with his friend, were the first native Ethiopians to become publishers. Before long one of them had 20 attending his home Bible studies. (66) Missionaries in Liberia had a very similar experience. (73)

A circuit servant in Finland made good use of the long delay incident to changing trains on his way to a circuit assembly by arranging for a public lecture. A young woman who heard the lecture was convinced of the truth, journeyed to the assembly city and was baptized at the assembly. (135)

In British Guiana a lone publisher will often place from 40 to 50 magazines in just a few hours of witnessing while traveling by boat or train. (94) If all would be thus alert to buy out the opportune time how much greater would be the witness given and how our average hours would increase!

YOUR SERVICE MEETING


• In order that we may be able to give God’s Word the first place in our lives personal organization is necessary. The following demonstrations are designed to portray the effect personal organization has on our house-to-house preaching and our success as Kingdom publishers.


SCENE I (7 minutes) Car group arrives at unassigned territory. Proper personal preparation is evident as the car captain dispatches the group into the field. He assigns each a portion of territory. He checks to see that everyone has a good supply of all necessary equipment. He mentions the suggestion in the “Informant” to put in a good day of house-to-house work and to make every effort to place literature during this special work. After the territory assignment has been fully covered and time permits, a portion of the day should be set aside to call back on those who have taken literature, manifested interest, or were not at home, to talk to these persons and study with them.

A new publisher is with them and one of the mature publishers suggests that he accompany him to a few doors. The two then make a call and demonstrate how to present the offer effectively. Now the mature publisher asks the new publisher if he would like to make a call alone. He does, and each goes to a different house.

SCENE II (3 minutes) Later in the day the new publisher hints that it is time to stop. The other calls to mind the Society’s suggestion to spend a portion of their day calling back on those persons who took literature previously. He explains that some who ■were not home earlier may have returned from church and can be contacted, also this form of service provides variety and increases time spent in service. The brother invites the new publisher to accompany him to make a call on a man with whom he could place only a magazine the last time. The two agree to go.

SCENE III (5 minutes) Call is made on the person where placement was made. The man is pleasant and invites them in. He says that he read the magazine and liked the clear presentation of Biblical subjects. He adds that he has always believed there was something to the Bible but now through this publication he realizes its value. He cannot understand, however, why so many people ignore it. The brother hands him a copy of “What Has Religion Done for Mankind?” and suggests they turn to page 26. They begin to read and look up supporting scriptures as they read along. The question is answered clearly and successfully and then the three books are presented and arrangements are made for a back-call. As they walk away the new publisher remarks how quickly the time went and perhaps more calls can be made after the public meeting. [Companies having no unassigned territory and those that have publishers working in home territory can adapt portions to fit needs, such as doing house-to-house work for two hours and then devoting one hour or so to calling back on literature placements or not-at-homes.]
