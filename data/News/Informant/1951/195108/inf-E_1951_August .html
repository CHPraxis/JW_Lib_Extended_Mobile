YOUR SERVICE MEETING


In times past, Jehovah’s witnesses have had the privilege of witnessing before many authorities of the lanti. By contending for our rights from the police court right on up, we have succeeded, by Jehovah’s undeserved kindness, in building a mass of precedent to protect our legal rights. However, the battle to keep the way open to preach the good news is not over, for the enemy is constantly striving to break down that which has been built up. This demonstration, therefore, is for the purpose of showing the need of preparedness when accosted by local policemen.


SCENE I: (4 minutes) An unprepared publisher is doing street magazine work. He Is accosted by a policeman and, after a brief discussion, is arrested.

The publisher, not having studied Defending and Legally Establishing the Good N'eics, is found standing in front of a store window. The policeman approaches him and explains that the manager complains that he is blocking the view and blocking the traffic, and wouldn’t he please move out to the curb. Immediately the publisher becomes excited, firmly announcing that he will not move and that he will stand on his constitutional rights. He quotes such scripture as “We must obey God rather than men” and' denounces man-made laws. The patient policeman tries to reason with the publisher, but finally loses his composure and gruffly places him under arrest.

SCENE II: (6 minutes) A well-prepared publisher is standing on the street and he too is accosted by an officer. After a calm discussion, he agrees to go down to the station and have a talk with the desk sergeant.

This publisher, having studied the legal booklet, is standing near the curb away from the store windows and traffic. While he is energetically presenting the magazines to passers-by, the officer approaches him and informs him that because of complaints raised, he will have to cease his work there. Calmly, the publisher suggests that perhaps the officer and complainants were misinformed concerning the legality of his work. While saying this, he brings out his copy of the legal booklet. He explains that there have already been a number of similar eases, and he points to a few of them in the legal booklet. The policeman is somewhat taken aback and seems a little uncertain of himself. The publisher further points out that as long as he is not blocking traffic or disturbing the peace, there certainly could be no valid objection to his being there. The publisher, being calm and sure of his legal standing, has partially convinced the policeman of his right to be there. But the officer is under orders, so he asks the publisher to go to the station with him and explain that to the desk sergeant. To this the publisher agrees.

SCENE III: (10 minutes) The publisher and the policeman are talking to the desk sergeant.

The policeman explains that this is the person against whom the complaint was registered, but after talking to him, he isn't sure that the publisher is doing anything unlawful. The desk sergeant then asks the publisher the nature of his work. Using the information on pages 7, 24 and 87 of the booklet Defending and Legally Establishing- the Good News. the publisher shows exactly what he was doing, why he was doing it and the Scriptural backing for it, and how he is trained to do it properly without creating a disturbance or violating the law. The desk sergeant raises three objections, which are answered by referring him to the court decisions listed on page 36 and in the appendix. The desk sergeant then agrees that the publisher should be released without charges being filed, and he returns to his street corner to resume his Kingdom service.
