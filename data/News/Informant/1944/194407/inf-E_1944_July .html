Theocratic Ministry Booklet

The Society Has No Branch for the Blind

Recently a letter was circulated throughout the country making a request for money and stating that the money should be sent to the Watch Tower Bible & Tract Society, Logansport, Ind. The letter stated that the money was to aid the ‘Blind Branch of the Society’. The Society does not have an office for the blind at Logansport. Ind. The request for funds to support this work for the blind was made without the knowledge of the Society. The facts are these:

On July 15, 1936, the president of the Society wrote the one handling the work for the blind that no further attempt would be made to print literature in Braille. Instructions were issued at that time that the material on hand should be used, debts paid, and the work be discontinued. Later in 1936 the 1937 | Yearbook of Jehovah's witnesses was published and on page 105. under the subheading “Blind”, this statement is made: “For some time the Society has maintained a small printing plant and depot for the benefit of the blind, at Logansport, Ind. There has been little progress in the work. . . . The opportunity for Jehovah’s witnesses in various places who can see and read is to read for those who are blinded and help them to understand the truth and comfort their hearts. Furthermore, the phonograph furnishes a means of testimony and they can hear In this manner. The interest manifested by the blind hardly seems to justify the continuing of this branch of the service, in view of the fact that they can receive the truth by having it read to them by others and by means of electrical transcription and phonograph machines.”

From that time forward the Society has not financially or otherwise supported work for the blind, nor has it directed the work that has been going on at Logansport, Ind. Some persons have continued to publish the Watchtower magazine and other publications in Braille at Logansport. They have done this work in the name of the Watch Tower, but not with the Watch Tower’s approval. Now someone has taken it upon himself to solicit money in the name of the Watch Tower for the support of this work. I This solicitation for money was made w’ithout authority from the Society and without the Society’s knowledge.

It is the Society’s suggestion that blind persons interested in the truth be taken to the service meetings, book studies and Watchtower studies and there receive the comforting message, as do all others of the j Lord's people. The blind should meet at the Kingdom Hall, just as the Greek, Polish, Italian and American brethren do, all at unity and working to the one end, namely, honoring the Lord’s name.

Stock Servant

The stock servant is the custodian of the actual medium by which the publishers make contact with the people in conveying to them the message of the Kingdom. The company stock Is the truth in printed form, and the sacred trust of properly caring for and dispensing it is committed into his hands. He will therefore be justly concerned in seeing that the literature is faithfully and zealously taken to the people, and will himself set a good example of diligent action in the field.

The immediate question of how much stock should be on hand will be settled not by ordering huge supplies that cannot be handled by the company, neither by frugally refraining from having sufficient variety and amounts to meet the publishers’ needs. Careful ordering in 100-lb. shipments whenever possible will insure a balanced supply of literature. If an oversupply has accumulated, the Society regularly provides special campaigns to allow the companies opportunity to clean out such stock. Here is where the alert stock servant will in co-operation with all others in the compan; make a determined effort to taki the literature which certainly i: helping no one by remaining on th, shelves and see that those needing it are supplied.

Aging, untidy, damp and dusty literature, carelessly and indifferently handled, is a reproach to a company; so the stock servant will, if possible, provide shelves and cupboards, etc., that can be locked and protected from dust and dampness. This will prevent deterioration and waste, and cause all those needing literature to come at certain specified times to obtain it. In this manner many company stock accounts will be better balanced. Extra supplies that must be left in cartons will also be kept from dust and dampness, and the contents itemized and marked on the cartons for easy counting and checking. Where the company stock servant needs aides these will be provided, and he will see that confusion does not exist by allowing everyone in the company to come and help himself with no record of such transactions kept. Record of all literature taken by the publishers will be kept on the Publisher's Order Blanks, totaled, and entered as the weekly movement of stock.

The use of the Progressive Inventory will enable the stock servant to know exactly how much stock is on hand, and its value. Where the amount of stock plus total cash on hand for literature, and value of literature held by publishers on credit equal the amount owed the Society, a healthy condition exists; but where the total value of stock and cash does not equal the amount owed, there is a deficit which should be remedied by the company.

The stock servant will see that all area book studies may have a small supply of literature, the book study conductor being responsible for its care. For all money that the stock servant turns in each week he will receive a credit memo from the accounts servant.

When shipments of literature are received from the Society an immediate check will be made, reporting any errors in invoicing or damage, so that a prompt adjustment can be made. An efficient stock servant will not stand idly by and watch stock accumulate and grow aged or unfit for use, nor watch the account become off balance and deficits mount. He cannot move the company's stock alone, but by the co-operation of all in the company such can be done.
