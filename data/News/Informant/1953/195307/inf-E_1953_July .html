Traffic and Parking

  • 7 Many thousands of persons will use every means of transportation to assemble with the New World society at Yankee Stadium. Room requests show 70 per cent will be coming by automobile. It is much better to allow plenty of time to travel than to wait until the last minute and depend on breakneck speeds to arrive on time. Slow down I Drive carefully!

  • 8 Upon arriving In New York city, watch traffic and parking signs. Parking regulations are strictly enforced. There are many one-way streets, and these should especially be watched. If you need directions in addition to those given you at the Society’s information booths (see June Informant for locations), it is best to ask a policeman. They are always courteous and helpful. The police department has suggested that, to avoid theft, cars should be locked at all times and valuables should never be left visible inside.

  • 9 There is very limited parking space near Yankee Stadium. That which is available is run by commercial garages and parking lots. These are quite expensive, generally $1 a day. If you patronize these garages or lots, check to see If the price covers twenty-four hours or If there is an extra charge after a certain time limit. Prices and time periods are posted, and these signs should be noted carefully.

  • 10 Most hotels and private home accommodations are easily accessible to Yankee Stadium by subway. With a ten-cent fare, you will appreciate that it is by far the cheapest, and it will generally be the fastest method of transportation. In harmony with this point, many will wish to park their car for the eight days of the assembly and ride the subway. For those who wish to park their car off the street during the assembly, arrangements are being made with a large open-air parking lot about three miles from the stadium. The cost will be approximately $3 for the eight or ten days, representing quite a saving over parking near the stadium. Parking tickets can be obtained at the Parking Department of the convention.

    YOUR SERVICE MEETING

    The first demonstration below emphasizes showing appreciation and kind consideration of others at the world assembly, so that no disturbance interferes with enjoyment of the assembly. The second demonstration deals with parental responsibility toward children in connection with the assembly. Consider both demonstrations before leaving for New York.


SCENE 1 (3 min.) At Yankee Stadium two publishers are sitting side by side listening to lecture. Daisy nudges Mabel, saying, “Mabel, Mabel! There's Jane sitting over there. You know, we have not seen her in three years. Det’s go over and say hello right now, because if we wait we will lose her in the crowd.” Mabel: “Sh-sh-sh-sh.” She tries to quiet Daisy and at the same time continue her own interest in the lecture. But Daisy says, “Well, if you are not going, I am anyway.” This scene is accompanied by waving and gesticulating by Daisy.

SCENE li (5 min.) Conductor points out commotion that was caused by Daisy and Mabel, disturbing those nearby. Finally Daisy reaches the section where Jane is sitting and goes through the same upsetting procedure of making people stand while she squeezes past to reach Jane. Then they carry on quite a conversation about not having seen one another for three years and what has been happening in each other’s life. Having lost the entire theme of the lecture by this time, and feeling it is about over anyway, Jane suggests they get up and head for the cafeteria. If they do not there will be such a crowd they will miss out on their meal.

An attendant sitting nearby and observing the entire disturbing commotion reminds them of the Society’s suggestion that publishers remain in their seats, quietly paying attention until the program is finished, rather than be in a big hurry to get to the cafeteria. He reminds them there will be plenty for all.

Jane begins to understand and show’s remorse about the entire episode and the fact that she caused so much disturbance. From now on she is determined to be mindful of not only her own comfort and desires, but also those of persons near her. In the meantime the meeting is dismissed and Daisy says, “You know, I think it is a good idea to remain in our seats for ten or fifteen minutes until the big rush is over. We can be comfortable and this will give us an opportunity to finish our little visit.” Time goes by quickly, and a half hour has elapsed. They are now ready to leave ana enjoy their meal at the cafeteria.

SCENE III (7 min.) Conductor summarizes by bringing out the points in July 1, 1953, “Watchtower” article “Showing Appreciation at Assemblies”. Mabel, Daisy, Jane and everybody in their vicinity would have profited by applying these suggestions. There would be no running around during sessions, no leaving before sessions are ended. Rather, there would be careful attention by all, making the assembly enjoyable and profitable to the full. (In summary, do not go beyond the subheading “Supervising Conduct of Children” in the “Watchtower” article, as this applies to the next demonstration.)

SCENE I (3 min.) Conductor gives brief talk about trip to convention, showing need for planning in every way. Condition of the family car, routes to be taken, economy, clothes, literature—all these things must be considered. But trip offers wonderful educational opportunities too. Children must get the most out of the trip in every way, en route and at the convention. Scenic and historic spots along the way will contribute toward education, but attention must be given to children at the assembly itself. They must be well-mannered and attentive to what is going on; and it is up to the parents to make this assembly a lasting impression on the child’s mind and to look after him.

SCENE II (8 min.) Father, mother and two children sit at table with maps, notebook and copy of July 1, 1953, “Watchtower.” They discuss routes to be taken and points of interest to be seen; what must be done to the car: what clothes they will take, etc. Children speak up and say what a good time they are going to have running around underneath the stadium stands, and around the cafeteria tents. They can even play games by running from one side of the stadium to the other in the stands. But father says “No !” There Is something more important for them to do, which is the reason for going all the way to New York. The way children act at -an .assembly is the way the parents let the children behave in their own Kingdom Hall. Bad children bring a reflection on the parents.

He takes out July 1 “Watchtower” and turns to article “Showing Appreciation at Assemblies”, the subheading “Supervising Conduct of Children”. A discussion ensues between father and mother, and with children, bringing out various points in “Watchtower” article. Some parts are read. The children are shown the reasonableness of the points made; how it will help them to become better servants of Jehovah; and how their conforming to the conduct outlined will help everyone else enjoy the convention. Parents must instruct children and then back up that instruction with firm supervision of their conduct. There will be plenty to see and hear anywhere in the stadium. Here is where the really lasting impressions must be made on the child’s mind.

SCENE III (4 min.) Father and mother, toward the latter part of the convention, are discussing success they have had in properly caring for their children They took them along wherever they went; had them sitting quietly during meetings; did not permit them to disturb others by running around or continually going in and out. They make mention of how busy this kept them, but the fruits of their labors have been well rewarded by noticing the benefits the children have received by paying close attention to lectures. Father remarks, “I never before realized the importance of keeping a close check on our children rather than permitting them to run at random.” He recalls the advice of “The Watchtower” that “the greatest heritage one can leave his children is the desire to become an active minister to Jehovah”. To this end, these faithful parents appreciate the importance of seeing that their children get all they can out of the convention.
