Literature Placements a Means to an End

Our Yearbook report shows that world-wide we placed over 18 million books and booklets, more than 76 million individual magazines and obtained more than one and a quarter million subscriptions during the 1957 service year. Why do we endeavor to place literature with the people? Because we want them to learn about Jehovah and his kingdom and get on the road that leads to life.

But for this literature to serve its purpose it must be read and understood. That means that we must call back and stimulate interest in the literature and then aid people to understand it. If we fail to do that we miss the very purpose of our placing the literature in the first place, which is thus seen to be merely a means to an end. The same is even true of our subscriptions. Unless we encourage them to read and help them to understand the magazines, it may be that after a few issues they won’t even take the trouble to remove the magazines from their wrappers. So, let us never forget, placing literature is only a means to an end.

Essential Qualities of Sermons Part 4-—Tactfulness

  • 1 Jesus, the great Exemplar, gave good counsel on one of the prime requisites for quality sermons, namely, tactfulness. He said: “Prove yourselves cautious as serpents and yet innocent as doves.” This meant that they must be tactful. Paul also declared : “But a slave of the Lord does not need to fight, but needs to be tactful toward all.” But precisely what does it mean to be tactful? Tactfulness may be defined as: a sensitive mental perception; or an acute, keen discernment of the best course of action to take under given conditions; or the peculiar ability to deal with others without giving offense. Tact is knowing what to say, how to say it and when to say it. —Matt. 10:16; 2 Tim. 2: 24.

  • 2 Hence, the importance and value of being tactful is seen at once. It has many benefits to offer. Its proper use will aid us to go far in giving our sermons and will help us to reach the objective. It will help us to get oui- information across to the householder without offending him and closing his mind to the message. Tactfulness should not be confused with compromise or deceit. We can go too far in. the matter of tact and become overtactful, servile, apologetic, even compromising the truth, which would be wrong, the same as being untactful. So strike a happy balance and use the right amount of tact at the proper time.

Supporting Kingdom Service

As dedicated Christian ministers all we have belongs to Jehovah. We are merely stewards, obligated to make the best possible use of everything entrusted to our care, not only of our time and strength, but also of our money. As noted in The Watchtower, March 1, 1955, we have privileges and obligations to give support to our local congregation, to the Society and to needy brothers, especially those serving full time. And until the big convention next summer, we all have an added privilege and obligation along this line, namely, to contribute toward bringing as many missionaries and other fulltime servants as possible to that convention.

  • 3 A few examples perhaps will aid us to see the point clearly. We may meet a householder who says, “I’m a Catholic and I’m not interested in your work.” You could counter with, “I’m glad to know that you are a Catholic. We have at least one thing in common—we both see the importance of spreading religion in this delinquent world.” Such a statement is both truthful and tactful. You are glad to know that he is a Catholic, for that will aid you to adapt your presentation to appeal to him and at the same time you acknowledge his objection.

  • 4 Another illustration would be the householder who says, “I’m busy.” In all sincerity you could say, “But you shouldn’t be too busy to listen to the message I have for'you.” That would be truthful, but not tactful. It would be better to tactfully acknowledge we live in a busy world and then point out that even in a busy world we must pause to think about God and his purposes.

  • 5 So always work for a tactful presentation. Think of what you say and how you say it. Think in terms of the householder’s reaction.
